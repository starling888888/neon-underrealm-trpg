# ゲーム画像生成用ベースプロンプト

Create a serious, grounded cyberpunk-noir illustration set in modern Japan, specifically Osaka.

Depict a dense urban environment at night: rain-soaked streets, aging buildings, narrow alleys, subdued neon, practical lighting, and visible traces of long-term human use. The city should feel oppressive, humid, enclosed, and lived-in.

Cyberpunk must not be presented as a flashy futuristic spectacle. Advanced technology should feel embedded in ordinary life: old shops, mixed-use buildings, back streets, housing, workshops, clinics, factories, religious spaces, and criminal businesses. Do not assemble a checklist of familiar cyberpunk symbols. Show the city as the result of technology gradually becoming part of daily life.

Use a restrained, cold palette built from black, grey, dark blue, faded brown, and desaturated colors. Neon should be sparse, functional, and localized. Avoid decorative glow, excessive holographic advertising, and high-saturation lights scattered across the entire image.

Lighting should be cinematic but realistic. Do not illuminate every object beautifully or conveniently. Clearly distinguish between lit areas, deep shadow, blocked light, reflected light, and spaces where illumination does not reach. Prioritize weight, stillness, humidity, and evidence of daily life over spectacle.

Do not give the entire image uniform detail density. Vary the amount of visual information, sharpness, and contrast between foreground, middle ground, and background. Preserve areas of visual rest: blank walls, closed shutters, unmarked doors, empty storefronts, stripped advertisement panels, dark gaps, and spaces with no narrative importance. Not every part of the image should explain the setting or function as a visual highlight.

Avoid an overly polished or mathematically balanced composition. Do not rely on perfect symmetry, a rigid central vanishing point, evenly spaced objects, or a textbook key-art layout. The scene should feel like an incidental slice of a real place, with asymmetry, obstruction, cropping, negative space, and imperfect visibility. It must still remain visually readable and suitable for a tabletop RPG website, cover, chapter opener, or world-guide image.

Architecture and infrastructure must be physically plausible. Pipes, cables, ducts, air-conditioning units, lights, cameras, terminals, and industrial devices must connect logically to buildings and systems. Avoid pipes that disappear without purpose, meaningless cable branches, unsupported machinery, objects merging into walls, or repeated equipment used as decorative texture.

Do not place small objects merely to fill empty space. Trash bags, crates, containers, bicycles, motorcycles, signs, tools, and street furniture should appear where they would reasonably be used or stored. Avoid evenly distributing identical props throughout the scene. Each object should have a believable purpose, weight, scale, and relationship to its surroundings.

Differentiate materials clearly. Wet asphalt, dry areas beneath awnings, light-absorbing concrete, damp wood and fabric, partially reflective metal, dirty glass, oil-stained pavement, and mud should behave differently under light. Do not make the entire image equally wet, glossy, sharp, or reflective.

Rain reflections must be irregular. Break reflections with puddles, drainage slopes, cracked pavement, seams, dirt, oil, obstacles, and dry patches. Avoid mirror-like streets or perfectly arranged colored reflections designed only to guide the viewer’s eye.

If characters are present, depict them as experienced, worn, and shaped by their lives. They must not appear heroic, idealized, glamorous, or theatrically posed. Favor quiet moments before or after action, waiting, walking, observing, or working.

Characters should not look directly at the viewer. Use side profiles, backs, lowered gazes, partially obscured faces, or silhouettes integrated into the environment. Expressions should be restrained, neutral, tired, guarded, or emotionally suppressed. Do not place distant figures in the exact center merely to create depth.

Cybernetics and future technology should be practical, industrial, heavy, worn, repaired, and integrated into everyday use. Avoid pristine science-fiction surfaces, glossy armor, ornamental glowing components, or technology designed only to look impressive. Include scratches, grime, repair marks, mismatched replacement parts, improvised modifications, and evidence of routine maintenance.

Technology should function as a tool of labor, medicine, commerce, surveillance, religion, organized crime, and violence. It should not dominate the image as an exhibition piece.

Japanese and Osaka-specific elements must not be presented as a tourist collage. Do not automatically combine lanterns, food stalls, kanji signs, towers, power lines, shrines, motorcycles, and neon in every scene. Select only the elements the location actually needs.

Express Osaka through street layout, building density, aging commercial architecture, shop fronts, practical clutter, local business culture, and the relationship between old infrastructure and new technology. Avoid theme-park Japan, travel-poster imagery, or an outsider’s collection of Japanese visual clichés.

If a landmark is included, allow buildings, wires, rain, haze, signage, and perspective to partially obscure it. Do not display every important object in full, unobstructed view. A landmark should feel like part of the city rather than a symbol placed for the viewer.

Do not include text, logos, titles, or typography unless they are explicitly requested.

When no exact text has been specified, signs and displays must contain no readable writing. Use blank panels, inactive screens, simple geometric symbols, pictograms, color blocks, or non-linguistic interface elements.

When exact text is explicitly specified, render only the requested strings and reproduce them exactly. Preserve every character, kanji, kana, spelling, word order, and punctuation mark. Do not invent additional words, substitute similar characters, create pseudo-Japanese, or add decorative lettering.

If an exact string cannot be rendered correctly, leave the sign blank rather than generating broken text.

Do not create text-like strokes or unreadable pseudo-writing on distant signs. Distant displays should use simple light segments, geometric patterns, pictograms, or abstract interface blocks.

Any specified text must follow the perspective, surface, material, weathering, and lighting of the object it is printed or displayed on. Text must not float independently in space.

Do not repeat multiple signs that explain the same business, technology, or setting concept. If unusual setting-specific language is used, balance it with ordinary commercial or everyday signage so the environment feels inhabited rather than presented as a setting exhibition.

Avoid common generative-image artifacts, including:

- unreadable pseudo-text
- duplicated signs, windows, vents, pipes, cables, or air-conditioning units
- repeated objects with nearly identical shapes
- objects merging into one another
- architecture that changes structure midway
- pipes and cables with no source or destination
- machinery with no believable function
- excessive decorative complexity
- evenly distributed clutter
- uniform sharpness across the entire image
- uniform wetness, gloss, texture, and lighting
- every object serving as worldbuilding exposition
- a complete checklist of cyberpunk motifs in one frame
- conveniently visible neon, rain, people, signs, and landmarks all at once
- distant figures placed only as depth markers
- unrealistically perfect road reflections
- an image that feels too polished, cinematic, or compositionally complete
- trash, boxes, cables, advertisements, and props added only to fill space

The overall tone must be mature, grounded, non-heroic, restrained, oppressive, and believable.

The city should feel quiet, heavy, damp, decayed, and inhabited. It must look like a place where people have lived, worked, fought, repaired equipment, reused buildings, and adapted to corruption over many years.

The final image should feel appropriate for an independent tabletop RPG cover, website hero image, chapter opener, or world-guide illustration.

Avoid anime exaggeration, comic-book posing, glossy science-fiction spectacle, tourism advertising, theme-park aesthetics, and overly dramatic promotional key art.
